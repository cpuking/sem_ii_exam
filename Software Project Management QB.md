## ⭐ 1. What is a Project? `IMPORTANT`

### Definition

A **project** is a **temporary endeavor** undertaken to create a **unique product, service, or result**. It has a defined start and end date, specific objectives, and consumes resources.

### Key Characteristics

|Characteristic|Description|Example|
|---|---|---|
|**Temporary**|Definite start and end|6-month app development|
|**Unique**|Not routine/repetitive work|Custom software for client|
|**Goal-oriented**|Specific objectives|Launch e-commerce website|
|**Resource-constrained**|Limited budget, time, people|₹10L budget, 5-person team|
|**Progressive elaboration**|Details refined over time|Requirements evolve with feedback|

### Project vs Operations

|Feature|Project|Operations|
|---|---|---|
|Duration|Temporary|Ongoing|
|Output|Unique|Repetitive|
|Team|Assembled for project|Permanent|
|Goal|Achieve objective, then end|Maintain business continuity|
|Example|Build new banking app|Maintain existing banking app|

### Project Examples in Software

- Developing a mobile application
- Migrating legacy system to cloud
- Implementing ERP system
- Building an e-commerce website
- Creating an AI-powered chatbot

> 📝 **3-mark answer:** Definition + 5 characteristics + project vs operations table.

## ⭐ 2. What is Project Management? `IMPORTANT`

### Definition

**Project Management** is the application of knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques to project activities to meet project requirements within defined constraints of scope, time, and cost.

### Five Process Groups (PMBOK)

|Process Group|Key Activities|Output|
|---|---|---|
|**Initiating**|Define project, get approval|Project Charter|
|**Planning**|Plan scope, schedule, cost|Project Management Plan|
|**Executing**|Perform the work|Deliverables|
|**Monitoring & Controlling**|Track progress, manage changes|Performance Reports|
|**Closing**|Formal acceptance, archive|Project Closure Report|

### Ten Knowledge Areas (PMBOK)

|#|Knowledge Area|Focus|
|---|---|---|
|1|Integration Management|Overall coordination|
|2|Scope Management|What is included/excluded|
|3|Schedule Management|Timelines and deadlines|
|4|Cost Management|Budget and expenditure|
|5|Quality Management|Standards and requirements|
|6|Resource Management|People and materials|
|7|Communications Management|Info distribution|
|8|Risk Management|Identify/mitigate risks|
|9|Procurement Management|Contracts and vendors|
|10|Stakeholder Management|Managing expectations|

### PM Skills Required

|Skill Type|Examples|
|---|---|
|Technical|Scheduling, budgeting, risk analysis|
|Leadership|Motivating team, resolving conflicts|
|Strategic|Aligning project to business goals|
|Communication|Reporting, presentations, meetings|

> 📝 **5-mark answer:** Definition + 5 process groups table + 5 key knowledge areas + skills.

## ⭐ 3. Systems View of Project Management `IMPORTANT`

### Definition

The **systems view** treats a project as a complete system where all components interact and affect each other. It emphasizes understanding the project in context of its larger environment.

### System Components

```
INPUTS                    PROCESSES                  OUTPUTS
──────────               ──────────                 ──────────
Requirements  ──────►   Planning        ──────►    Deliverables
Resources              Execution                   Reports
Budget                 Monitoring                  Documentation
Constraints            Control                     
Stakeholder needs      Communication              

                        FEEDBACK LOOP
                    (Monitoring feeds back to Planning)
```

### Why Systems View Matters

|Benefit|Explanation|
|---|---|
|Holistic thinking|See all components and their interactions|
|Risk reduction|Understand ripple effects of changes|
|Better decisions|Consider system-wide impact|
|Change management|Assess how changes affect whole system|

### Systems Approach in Practice

- If requirements change → assess impact on schedule AND cost AND quality
- If team member leaves → reassign tasks, adjust timeline, manage risk
- If budget cut → reduce scope OR extend timeline OR reduce quality

## ⭐ 4. Organizational Structures `IMPORTANT`

### Definition

The **organizational structure** determines how authority, resources, and communication flow in a project.

### Types of Structures

|Type|Description|PM Authority|Resource Control|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Functional**|Organized by department (IT, HR, Finance)|Little/None|Functional Manager|
|**Projectized**|Organized by project|High/Total|Project Manager|
|**Weak Matrix**|Functional base + PM coordinator|Limited|Functional Manager|
|**Balanced Matrix**|Equal PM & Functional Manager power|Mixed|Shared|
|**Strong Matrix**|PM has more authority|Moderate-High|PM mostly|
|**Composite**|Mix of above|Varies|Varies|

### Comparison: Functional vs Projectized vs Matrix

|Feature|Functional|Matrix|Projectized|
|---|---|---|---|
|PM Role|Part-time coordinator|Shared authority|Full authority|
|Team loyalty|To department|Split|To PM/project|
|Resource flexibility|Low|Medium|High|
|Career path|Clear within dept|Complex|Only project-based|
|Best for|Routine, stable work|Balanced needs|Large, complex projects|
|Example|Government orgs|Large IT companies|Construction firms|

## ⭐ 6. Triple Constraint (Iron Triangle) `VERY IMPORTANT`

### Definition

The **Triple Constraint** states that every project is constrained by three interdependent factors: **Scope**, **Time**, and **Cost**. Changing one always affects the others.

### The Iron Triangle

```
             SCOPE
            ╱    ╲
           ╱  QUALITY  ╲
          ╱   (center)   ╲
         ╱─────────────────╲
      TIME ─────────────── COST
```

**Quality** sits at the center — it is the result of balancing all three.

### How Changes Affect Each Other

|Change|Impact|
|---|---|
|Increase scope →|Increase time AND/OR cost|
|Reduce time →|Increase cost (more resources) OR reduce scope|
|Reduce cost →|Reduce scope OR extend timeline|
|Increase quality →|Increase time AND cost|

### Examples

|Scenario|Triple Constraint Implication|
|---|---|
|Client adds new features mid-project|Scope ↑ → Time ↑ and/or Cost ↑|
|Management wants earlier deadline|Time ↓ → Cost ↑ (more staff) or Scope ↓|
|Budget is cut by 20%|Cost ↓ → Scope ↓ or Time ↑|

> 📝 **3-mark answer:** Definition + triangle diagram + 3 examples of how changes propagate.

## ⭐ 7. Scope Management `IMPORTANT`

### Definition

**Scope Management** ensures the project includes all the work required — and only the work required — to complete it successfully.

### Processes

|Process|Description|Output|
|---|---|---|
|**Scope Planning**|How scope will be defined, managed, controlled|Scope Management Plan|
|**Scope Definition**|Detailed description of project and product|Scope Statement|
|**Create WBS**|Decompose deliverables into smaller tasks|WBS, WBS Dictionary|
|**Scope Verification**|Stakeholders formally accept deliverables|Accepted deliverables|
|**Scope Control**|Manage changes to scope baseline|Change requests|

### Scope Creep

**Definition:** Unauthorized gradual expansion of project scope without adjustments to schedule or budget.

**Prevention:**

- Document requirements thoroughly in Scope Statement
- Use formal change control process
- Get stakeholder sign-off on initial scope
- Track scope baseline vs current scope

## ⭐ 8. Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) `IMPORTANT`

### Definition

A **WBS** is a hierarchical decomposition of the project scope into smaller, manageable components called **work packages**.

### WBS for an E-Commerce Website

```
E-Commerce Website (Level 0)
├── 1. Frontend Development (Level 1)
│   ├── 1.1 Homepage Design (Level 2)
│   ├── 1.2 Product Catalog Pages
│   ├── 1.3 Shopping Cart
│   └── 1.4 Checkout Flow
├── 2. Backend Development
│   ├── 2.1 User Authentication API
│   ├── 2.2 Product Management API
│   └── 2.3 Order Processing API
├── 3. Database Design
│   ├── 3.1 Schema Design
│   └── 3.2 Data Migration
├── 4. Testing
│   ├── 4.1 Unit Testing
│   ├── 4.2 Integration Testing
│   └── 4.3 UAT
└── 5. Deployment
    ├── 5.1 Server Setup
    └── 5.2 Production Release
```

### WBS Rules

|Rule|Explanation|
|---|---|
|100% rule|WBS must include 100% of project scope|
|Mutually exclusive|No overlap between work packages|
|Work packages|Lowest level should be estimable and assignable|
|8/80 rule|Work package: 8 hours minimum, 80 hours maximum|

### WBS vs Project Schedule

|WBS|Project Schedule|
|---|---|
|What needs to be done|When it will be done|
|Hierarchical structure|Timeline (Gantt chart)|
|No dates|Has start and end dates|
|Basis for schedule|Built from WBS|

> 📝 **5-mark answer:** Definition + WBS diagram + 100% rule + work package explanation.

## ⭐ 9. Time Management `IMPORTANT`

### Definition

**Project Time Management** involves the processes to manage timely completion of the project.

### Processes

|Process|Description|Tool|
|---|---|---|
|Activity Definition|Identify specific tasks from WBS|Activity list|
|Activity Sequencing|Determine order/dependencies|Network diagram, PDM|
|Duration Estimation|Estimate time per activity|PERT, Expert judgment|
|Schedule Development|Create project schedule|Gantt chart, CPM|
|Schedule Control|Monitor and manage changes|EVM, Crashing|

### Gantt Chart

```
Task            Jan   Feb   Mar   Apr
─────────────── ───── ───── ───── ─────
Requirements    ████
Design               ████ ██
Development               ████████
Testing                         ████
Deployment                           ██
```

### Critical Path Method (CPM)

- **Critical Path:** Longest path through the project network (determines minimum project duration)
- **Float/Slack:** Amount of time a task can be delayed without affecting project end date
- Tasks on critical path have **zero float**

### PERT (Program Evaluation Review Technique)

```
Expected Time = (Optimistic + 4 × Most Likely + Pessimistic) / 6

Example:
- Optimistic (O) = 4 days
- Most Likely (M) = 6 days
- Pessimistic (P) = 14 days
- PERT = (4 + 4×6 + 14) / 6 = (4 + 24 + 14) / 6 = 42/6 = 7 days
```

> 📝 **5-mark answer:** Definition + processes table + Gantt chart example + CPM definition + PERT formula with example.

## ⭐ 10. Cost Management `IMPORTANT`

### Definition

**Cost Management** includes processes to plan, estimate, budget, and control costs to complete the project within the approved budget.

### Processes

|Process|Description|Output|
|---|---|---|
|**Cost Estimation**|Estimate cost of each activity|Cost estimates|
|**Cost Budgeting**|Aggregate estimates to baseline|Cost baseline, BAC|
|**Cost Control**|Monitor and control cost variances|Forecasts, updates|

### Estimation Techniques

|Technique|Description|Accuracy|When Used|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Analogous**|Based on similar past projects|±25-50%|Early stages|
|**Bottom-up**|Estimate each task, roll up|±5-10%|Detailed planning|
|**Parametric**|Statistical relationship (e.g., $/line of code)|±10-20%|When data available|
|**Three-point (PERT)**|O + 4M + P / 6|±15-20%|Uncertain tasks|
|**Expert judgment**|Experienced person's estimate|Varies|Complex tasks|

### Earned Value Management (EVM)

|Metric|Formula|Meaning|
|---|---|---|
|**PV** (Planned Value)|Budgeted work scheduled|What should have been done|
|**EV** (Earned Value)|Budgeted work completed|What was actually accomplished|
|**AC** (Actual Cost)|Actual money spent|What was actually spent|
|**CV** (Cost Variance)|EV - AC|Positive = under budget|
|**SV** (Schedule Variance)|EV - PV|Positive = ahead of schedule|
|**CPI** (Cost Performance Index)|EV / AC|>1 = efficient, <1 = over budget|
|**SPI** (Schedule Performance Index)|EV / PV|>1 = ahead, <1 = behind|
|**EAC** (Estimate at Completion)|BAC / CPI|Projected final cost|

> 📝 **5-mark answer:** Definition + estimation techniques table + EVM metrics table with formulas.

## ⭐ 11. Quality Planning, QA, and QC `VERY IMPORTANT`

### Definitions

|Term|Definition|Focus|
|---|---|---|
|**Quality Planning**|Identifying quality standards relevant to the project and how to satisfy them|Prevention|
|**Quality Assurance (QA)**|Auditing quality requirements and results from quality control measurements to ensure standards are used|Process|
|**Quality Control (QC)**|Monitoring and recording results of quality activities to assess performance and recommend changes|Product|

### QA vs QC Comparison (Very Frequently Asked)

|Feature|Quality Assurance (QA)|Quality Control (QC)|
|---|---|---|
|Focus|**Process**|**Product**|
|Goal|**Prevent** defects|**Detect** defects|
|Type|Proactive|Reactive|
|When|Throughout project|After product is built|
|Who|QA team, auditors|QC team, testers|
|Activities|Process audits, standards, training|Testing, inspection, code review|
|Output|Process improvements|Defect reports, rework|
|Example|"Are developers following coding standards?"|"Does this feature work as specified?"|

### Quality Tools

|Tool|Type|Purpose|
|---|---|---|
|**Cause & Effect (Fishbone)**|Analysis|Find root cause of defects|
|**Control Charts**|Monitoring|Track process stability|
|**Pareto Chart**|Prioritization|80/20 – focus on major causes|
|**Flowcharts**|Documentation|Map processes|
|**Checksheets**|Data collection|Track defect types/frequency|
|**Scatter Diagrams**|Correlation|Find relationships between variables|
|**Histograms**|Distribution|Show frequency of occurrences|

### Six Sigma in Software

- **Goal:** Reduce defects to 3.4 per million opportunities
- **DMAIC:** Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control
- **Sigma levels:** 3σ = 93.3%, 6σ = 99.9997% quality

### Quality Metrics Examples

|Metric|Formula|Target|
|---|---|---|
|Defect density|Defects / KLOC|Lower is better|
|Defect removal efficiency|Defects found before release / Total defects|>90%|
|Test coverage|% of code covered by tests|>80%|
|Mean time to failure|Total operating time / # failures|Higher is better|

> 📝 **7-mark answer:** All three definitions + QA vs QC comparison table + quality tools + one metric formula.

## ⭐ 14. Stakeholder Management `VERY IMPORTANT`

### Definition

|Type|Description|Examples|
|---|---|---|

|**External**|Outside the organization|Clients, users, vendors, regulators|

  (Low       │       INFORMED
   both)     │       (High interest
             │        Low power)

|Quadrant|Strategy|Example|
|---|---|---|

|---|---|---|
|**Product metrics**|Attributes of the software|Size, complexity, reliability, maintainability|
|**Process metrics**|Software development process|Defect removal efficiency, review effectiveness|

## ⭐ 17. Size-Oriented Metrics (LOC) `IMPORTANT`

### Definition

**Size-oriented metrics** use **Lines of Code (LOC)** as the primary measure of software size.

### Derived Metrics Using LOC

|Metric|Formula|Example|
|---|---|---|
|Productivity|LOC / Person-month|1000 LOC / 2 person-months = 500|
|Cost|Total cost / LOC|₹5,00,000 / 10,000 LOC = ₹50/LOC|
|Defect density|Defects / KLOC|15 defects / 10 KLOC = 1.5 defects/KLOC|
|Documentation ratio|Doc pages / KLOC|—|

### Advantages and Disadvantages of LOC

|Advantages|Disadvantages|
|---|---|
|Simple to count|Language-dependent (Java vs Python)|
|Objective|Doesn't measure quality|
|Universal concept|Can encourage "padding" code|
|Historical basis|Comments and blanks create ambiguity|

### LOC Variants

- **SLOC (Source Lines of Code):** Only executable lines
- **LLOC (Logical LOC):** Count statements, not physical lines
- **BLOC (Blank LOC):** Blank lines
- **CLOC (Comment LOC):** Comment lines

---

## ⭐ 18. Function-Oriented Metrics (Function Points) `IMPORTANT`

### Definition

**Function Points (FP)** measure the functional size of software based on what the system does for the user, independent of programming language.

### Function Point Components

|Component|Abbrev|Example|
|---|---|---|
|External Inputs (EI)|Inputs|Login form, search query|
|External Outputs (EO)|Outputs|Reports, screen displays|
|External Inquiries (EQ)|Queries|Database queries, searches|
|Internal Logical Files (ILF)|Files|Database tables|
|External Interface Files (EIF)|External data|API calls to external systems|

### FP Calculation Steps

**Step 1:** Count each component and assign complexity weight

|Component|Simple|Average|Complex|
|---|---|---|---|
|EI|3|4|6|
|EO|4|5|7|
|EQ|3|4|6|
|ILF|7|10|15|
|EIF|5|7|10|

**Step 2:** Calculate Unadjusted Function Points (UFP)

```
UFP = Σ (count × weight) for all components
```

**Step 3:** Apply Value Adjustment Factor (VAF) based on 14 general system characteristics (complexity, performance, reusability, etc.) rated 0–5.

```
VAF = 0.65 + (0.01 × Σ complexity scores)
FP = UFP × VAF
```

### FP vs LOC

|Feature|Function Points|LOC|
|---|---|---|
|Language independence|✅ Yes|❌ No|
|Measurable early|✅ From requirements|❌ Only after coding|
|Understands complexity|✅ Yes|❌ No|
|Effort to count|More effort|Less effort|
|Industry acceptance|High|Very high|

> 📝 **5-mark answer:** Definition + components table + calculation steps + FP vs LOC comparison.

## ⭐ 19. Capability Maturity Model (CMM) `VERY IMPORTANT`

### Definition

**CMM (Capability Maturity Model)** is a framework developed by the **Software Engineering Institute (SEI) at Carnegie Mellon University** that describes the maturity of an organization's software development processes.

### Five Maturity Levels

|Level|Name|Description|Key Focus|Organization Type|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|**1**|**Initial**|Unpredictable, reactive, chaotic|None (ad hoc)|"Fire-fighting" culture|
|**2**|**Managed**|Project-level control, repeatable|Project management|Basic planning, tracking|
|**3**|**Defined**|Org-wide standard processes|Process standardization|Process documentation|
|**4**|**Quantitatively Managed**|Measured and controlled|Statistical process control|Metrics-driven|
|**5**|**Optimizing**|Continuous process improvement|Innovation, defect prevention|Learning organization|

### CMM Level Details

```
LEVEL 5 – OPTIMIZING
    ▲  Continuous improvement, defect prevention
    │
LEVEL 4 – QUANTITATIVELY MANAGED
    │  Measured processes, statistical control, predictable
    │
LEVEL 3 – DEFINED
    │  Standard org-wide processes, documented, consistent
    │
LEVEL 2 – MANAGED
    │  Project tracked, requirements managed, repeatable
    │
LEVEL 1 – INITIAL
       Ad hoc, chaotic, hero-dependent, unpredictable
```

### Key Process Areas (KPAs) per Level

|Level|KPAs|
|---|---|
|Level 2|Requirements Management, Project Planning, Project Tracking, Subcontract Management, QA, Configuration Management|
|Level 3|Organization Process Focus, Training Program, Integrated Software Management, Software Product Engineering, Peer Reviews|
|Level 4|Quantitative Process Management, Software Quality Management|
|Level 5|Defect Prevention, Technology Change Management, Process Change Management|

### CMMI (Successor to CMM)

- **CMMI = CMM Integration** – combines multiple CMM models
- Adds two representations: **Staged** (5 levels) and **Continuous** (0–5 per process area)
- Current version: CMMI v2.0

## ⭐ 20. PSP vs TSP `IMPORTANT`

### PSP – Personal Software Process

**Definition:** A structured framework developed by Watts Humphrey (SEI) for individual software engineers to improve their personal software development practices.

**Goal:** Help individual developers write better code with fewer defects, more accurate estimates, and improved personal productivity.

**PSP Levels:**

|Level|Name|Focus|
|---|---|---|
|PSP 0|Baseline|Measure current performance|
|PSP 0.1|Add coding standard|Code quality|
|PSP 1|Size estimation|Predict task size|
|PSP 1.1|Schedule planning|Plan personal time|
|PSP 2|Design and code reviews|Defect removal|
|PSP 2.1|Design templates|Systematic design|
|PSP 3|Cyclic development|Build iteratively|

### TSP – Team Software Process

**Definition:** A structured framework for building and managing high-performance software teams using data-driven methods.

**Goal:** Help software teams plan and track work, manage quality, and achieve project goals predictably.

### PSP vs TSP Comparison

|Feature|PSP|TSP|
|---|---|---|
|Level|Individual developer|Team|
|Focus|Personal work habits|Team coordination|
|Metrics|Individual performance|Team performance|
|Planning|Personal schedule|Team plan|
|Reviews|Self-reviews|Peer and team reviews|
|Goal|Personal quality improvement|Team delivery improvement|
|Foundation|PSP skills required first|Built on top of PSP|
|Time tracking|By individual|Aggregated from individuals|

### How PSP and TSP Work Together

```
Individual uses PSP
       ↓
Improves personal estimation, defect prevention
       ↓
Joins a team using TSP
       ↓
Team aggregates PSP data for team planning
       ↓
High-performance, predictable software delivery
```

## ⭐ 21. Risk Management `IMPORTANT`

### Definition

**Risk Management** is the systematic process of identifying, analyzing, responding to, and monitoring potential threats (risks) and opportunities in a project.

### Risk vs Issue

||Risk|Issue|
|---|---|---|
|Timing|Future – hasn't happened yet|Present – happening now|
|Management|Proactive (prevent/mitigate)|Reactive (resolve)|
|Certainty|Uncertain (probability < 100%)|Certain (already occurring)|

### Risk Management Process

|Process|Description|Output|
|---|---|---|
|**Identify**|List all potential risks|Risk Register|
|**Qualitative Analysis**|Prioritize risks by probability & impact|Risk priority ranking|
|**Quantitative Analysis**|Numerical analysis of high-priority risks|Expected Monetary Value|
|**Plan Response**|Define strategies to address risks|Risk Response Plan|
|**Implement Response**|Execute risk responses|Actions taken|
|**Monitor & Control**|Track risks, identify new risks|Risk updates|

### Risk Response Strategies

|Strategy|Type|Description|Example|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Avoid**|Threat|Eliminate the risk by changing plan|Drop risky feature|
|**Transfer**|Threat|Shift impact to third party|Insurance, outsourcing|
|**Mitigate**|Threat|Reduce probability or impact|Prototyping, reviews|
|**Accept**|Threat|Acknowledge and deal if occurs|Budget contingency|
|**Exploit**|Opportunity|Make opportunity more likely|Assign best resource|
|**Share**|Opportunity|Partner to better capture benefit|Joint venture|
|**Enhance**|Opportunity|Increase probability/impact|Increase resources|

### Risk Register Template

|ID|Risk Description|Category|Probability|Impact|Score|Response|Owner|
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
|R1|Key developer leaves|HR|Medium|High|High|Mitigation: documentation, cross-training|PM|
|R2|Requirement changes|Scope|High|High|Very High|Avoid: formal change control|BA|
|R3|Third-party API unavailable|Technical|Low|Medium|Medium|Transfer: SLA contract|Tech Lead|

## ⭐ 22. Communications Management `IMPORTANT`

### Definition

**Communications Management** ensures timely and appropriate generation, collection, distribution, storage, retrieval, and disposition of project information.

### Why Communication Matters

- 90% of a PM's time is spent on communication
- Most project failures trace back to communication breakdown
- Different stakeholders need different information at different times

### Communication Methods

|Method|Type|Best For|
|---|---|---|
|**Interactive**|Real-time, bidirectional|Complex issues (meetings, calls)|
|**Push**|One-way, directed|Status reports, newsletters, emails|
|**Pull**|Receiver initiates|Shared documents, wikis, portals|

### Communication Channels Formula

```
Number of channels = n(n-1)/2
where n = number of stakeholders

Example: 5 stakeholders = 5(5-1)/2 = 10 channels
         10 stakeholders = 10(9)/2 = 45 channels
         15 stakeholders = 15(14)/2 = 105 channels
```

This shows why **adding more people to a project** increases communication complexity exponentially!

### Information Distribution

**Goal:** Ensure right information reaches the right person at the right time in the right format.

|Element|Example|
|---|---|
|What|Project status, issues, decisions|
|Who|Sponsor, team, client, stakeholders|
|When|Weekly, milestone-based, on-demand|
|How|Email, dashboard, meeting, report|
|Format|Brief summary, detailed report, visual|

### Performance Reporting Types

|Report|Content|Audience|Frequency|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Status Report**|Current state (scope, schedule, cost, risks)|Sponsor, PMO|Weekly|
|**Progress Report**|Work completed vs planned|Team, stakeholders|Weekly/biweekly|
|**Forecast Report**|Future performance predictions (EAC, ETC)|Sponsor|Monthly|
|**Variance Report**|Deviations from plan (CV, SV)|PMO, management|Monthly|
